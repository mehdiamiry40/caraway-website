Old Car vs New Car Running Costs: When Should You Sell?

Published: January 18, 2025 8 min read

That old Commodore or Camry has been faithful for years, but lately it seems like you're at the mechanic every other month. Is it still worth keeping, or would you be better off selling for cash and starting fresh? Let's break down the real costs.

The True Cost of Running an Old Car

Most people only consider fuel and rego when thinking about car costs. But the real picture includes:

1. Maintenance & Repairs

As cars age, repair frequency and costs increase dramatically:

Vehicle Age Average Annual Repair Cost
0-3 years $200 - $500
4-7 years $600 - $1,200
8-12 years $1,500 - $3,000
12+ years $2,500 - $5,000+

Common expensive repairs on older Brisbane cars:

  • Timing belt replacement: $800 - $1,500
  • Transmission repair: $2,000 - $5,000
  • Air conditioning regas/repair: $300 - $1,200 (essential in Brisbane summers!)
  • Suspension overhaul: $1,000 - $2,500
  • Head gasket: $1,500 - $3,000

2. Fuel Efficiency

Older cars typically use more fuel due to:

  • Less efficient engine technology
  • Worn components reducing efficiency
  • Heavier weight (pre-lightweight materials era)

A 2008 Holden Commodore averages 11.5L/100km. A 2020 equivalent uses about 7.5L/100km. At Brisbane fuel prices (~$1.80/L), that's $1,400 extra per year for a typical 20,000km driver.

3. Registration & Insurance

While rego costs are similar regardless of age, insurance can vary:

  • Older cars = lower comprehensive premiums
  • But less coverage value if written off
  • Many owners switch to third-party only, accepting the risk

4. The "Unreliability Tax"

Hard to quantify but very real:

  • Missed work days due to breakdowns
  • Uber/taxi costs when car is in the shop
  • Stress and mental load of wondering "will it start?"
  • Embarrassment factor (we've all been there)

The 50% Rule: When to Sell

Industry experts use this guideline:

If annual repair costs exceed 50% of the car's value, it's time to sell.

Example: Your car is worth $4,000. If you're spending more than $2,000/year on repairs, you're throwing money away.
